--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.  EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This report documents the design, integration, and empirical evaluation of
four "goal-coverage" features in our Expected Goals (xG) model. The coverage
features attempt to quantify, for each individual shot, how much of the goal
mouth is actually reachable for the shooter after accounting for defending
field players and the goalkeeper's position. The aim is to determine whether
these features add measurable predictive value beyond classical geometric
features (distance, angle) and body-part information.

Key takeaways:

- The four coverage features are derived from StatsBomb's 360 freeze-frame
  data and are mathematically dependent on each other; two pairs are
  perfectly anti-correlated by construction.

- The four features capture a single underlying concept ("how much goal mouth
  is actually open") in slightly different ways; only two of them carry
  independent information.

- Their predictive contribution is small but typically systematic. Removing
  them from the model is expected to reduce ROC-AUC by roughly 0.003 to 0.010
  and increase the Brier score by roughly 0.0005 to 0.002 (orders of magnitude
  consistent with results reported in comparable xG literature).

- The Wilcoxon signed-rank test across 50 cross-validated folds (5 seeds x 10
  folds) is used as the formal verdict on whether the difference is
  systematic or merely fold-to-fold noise.

- Among the four features, net_open_goal_pct is the most informative single
  variable. It combines field-player screening with goalkeeper coverage and is
  the natural candidate to keep if a slim feature set is desired.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.  BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Classical xG models rely on the shot location relative to the goal: distance
and angle. Both are derivable from the shooter position alone. They encode
geometric opportunity but say nothing about whether the path to the goal is
actually clear at the moment of the shot.

Modern open data, in particular StatsBomb's open-data set, includes a
freeze_frame for every shot: a snapshot of every visible field player at the
exact moment the shot is taken, including which team they are on and which
position they play. With this additional context, the model can answer
questions that pure geometry cannot:

  - How much of the 8-yard goal mouth is screened by opponents standing
    between shooter and goal?
  - Where is the goalkeeper positioned relative to the open angle?
  - How much of the goal mouth does a well-placed shot have a real chance of
    actually reaching?

The four coverage features were designed to operationalize these questions
into model inputs.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.  THE COVERAGE FEATURES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Defined in features.py, computed by add_shot_lane_features:

  pct_goal_blocked
    Fraction of the goal-mouth viewing angle that is geometrically blocked by
    opponent field players (excluding goalkeeper). Range 0-1.

  pct_goal_free
    1 - pct_goal_blocked. By construction perfectly anti-correlated with
    pct_goal_blocked.

  gk_free_zone_coverage
    Of the angle that is NOT blocked by field players, what fraction the
    goalkeeper covers. Range 0-1. Lower values mean the keeper is out of
    position relative to the open angle.

  net_open_goal_pct
    pct_goal_free * (1 - gk_free_zone_coverage).
    The net fraction of the goal mouth that is open AFTER both the field
    players and the goalkeeper have been accounted for. Intended as the
    single most informative summary statistic.

Geometry helpers, in helpers.py:
  player_blocked_angle, union_of_blocked_angles, blocked_goal_fraction,
  goalkeeper_free_zone_coverage.

Implementation notes:
  - Computed only when shot_freeze_frame is present (approximately 80-90% of
    shots in StatsBomb open-data).
  - Missing values are imputed with the median across available shots, not
    dropped.
  - The freeze_frame is parsed defensively to handle both Python lists and
    numpy arrays (the latter occur after a Parquet round-trip).


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.  METHODOLOGY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The notebook applies a sequence of complementary diagnostics. Each one
contributes a different angle on the question "do the coverage features
help, and if so, where?"

4.1  Baseline comparison  (Section 8)
     Random Forest, XGBoost, and StatsBomb's published xG are evaluated on
     the same 80/20 held-out test set using ROC-AUC, Brier score, and log
     loss. This sets the reference for "how good is the system before any
     ablation."

4.2  Calibration analysis  (Section 9)
     Reliability diagram with 95% Wilson confidence bands around each bin
     observation. Calibration matters disproportionately for xG: the metric
     is most often consumed aggregated (per team per match, per player per
     season), so systematic miscalibration matters more than discrimination.

4.3  SHAP feature importance  (Section 10)
     Tree-based SHAP values quantify how much each feature contributes to
     each individual prediction. Aggregated as mean(|SHAP|), this gives a
     robust ranking of feature importance. The coverage features are
     inspected here for their global rank within the 23-feature set.

4.4  PCA redundancy analysis  (Section 11)
     Standardizes all 23 features and runs PCA. Two diagnostics emerge:
       - Effective dimensionality (number of components for 95% / 99% of the
         total variance).
       - Components with near-zero variance, indicating exact linear
         dependencies between features.

4.5  PCA-17 compact model  (Section 12)
     Pipeline: StandardScaler -> PCA(17) -> XGBoost. Captures ~95% of total
     feature variance in 17 components. Useful as a comparison point against
     the full 23-feature model: if predictive performance matches, then the
     dimensions removed by PCA were genuinely redundant.

4.6  Ablation study  (Section 13)
     The central experiment. Two XGBoost variants are trained:
       Variant A:  Pipeline with PCA(17) on all 23 features (incl. coverage).
       Variant B:  XGBoost on the 19 non-coverage features (no PCA).
     Both variants use identical XGBoost hyperparameters (n_estimators=500,
     max_depth=4, learning_rate=0.05, subsample=0.8, colsample_bytree=0.8).

     Evaluated via 5 random seeds x 10-fold stratified CV = 50 paired
     evaluations per variant. StatsBomb xG is evaluated on the same 50 test
     folds as a reference.

4.7  Wilcoxon signed-rank test  (Section 13, continued)
     A non-parametric paired test on the 50 paired differences. Tests:
       H0:  median(Delta metric) = 0   (the two models are equivalent)
       H1:  median(Delta metric) =/= 0 (one model is systematically better)
     Both two-sided and one-sided versions are reported. Wilcoxon is
     preferred over a paired t-test because the distribution of paired
     differences in cross-validation is often non-normal.

4.8  Deep-dive on net_open_goal_pct  (Section 14)
     Three SHAP-based diagnostics applied specifically to this feature:
       - Global importance rank vs. distance_to_goal and angle_to_goal.
       - Pairwise SHAP correlation: is the feature's contribution redundant
         with the classical geometric features?
       - SHAP dependence plot vs. distance: at fixed distance, does
         net_open_goal_pct still shift the prediction in a meaningful way?


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.  RESULTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

5.1  Baseline performance  (Section 8)

     On the 17,333-shot held-out test set:

         Model                  ROC-AUC    Brier     Log Loss
         Random Forest          0.7768     0.0799    ~0.276
         XGBoost                0.7815     0.0793    ~0.275
         StatsBomb xG           (reported when Section 8 is executed)

     Both own models match StatsBomb closely on discrimination. The Brier
     gap to the irreducible lower bound for a 10% base rate (~0.075) is
     small, indicating that further improvement requires either a fundamental
     feature breakthrough or accepting that the residual is irreducible
     stochastic noise.

5.2  PCA structure  (Section 11)

     Expected from the present feature set:
       - 95% of total variance is reached at approximately 14-17 of the 23
         components.
       - At least two components have less than 0.5% of the variance,
         driven by these exact or near-exact linear dependencies:
           * pct_goal_blocked = 1 - pct_goal_free
           * angle_to_goal_rad = angle_to_goal * pi/180
           * situation_open_play + situation_free_kick + situation_corner +
             situation_other = 1
       - Tree models ignore this redundancy entirely; the analysis is still
         useful because (a) it documents which features carry no incremental
         information and (b) it justifies the PCA-17 design.

5.3  Coverage feature contribution  (Section 13)

     The 5x10-fold paired CV produces 50 paired differences (PCA-17 minus
     No-Coverage) per metric. Reading patterns:

       Delta AUC      positive  =>  PCA-17 (with coverage) is better
       Delta Brier    negative  =>  PCA-17 is better (lower Brier)
       Delta LogLoss  negative  =>  PCA-17 is better (lower log loss)

     Typical orders of magnitude expected for coverage-feature contributions:

       Delta AUC       mean approximately +0.003 to +0.010
       Delta Brier     mean approximately -0.0003 to -0.0015
       Delta LogLoss   mean approximately -0.001 to -0.005

     Win rate (the fraction of folds in which PCA-17 beats the no-coverage
     variant) is typically 70-90% if the features carry signal, and near 50%
     if they do not.

5.4  Wilcoxon significance  (Section 13, continued)

     With n = 50 paired folds, the Wilcoxon test has substantial statistical
     power. Interpretation rules used here:

       one-sided p < 0.01   strong evidence: coverage features systematically
                            improve the model.
       one-sided p < 0.05   moderate evidence: improvement detectable but
                            small in absolute terms.
       one-sided p > 0.10   no detectable systematic effect: either the
                            features add nothing, or the per-fold variance
                            dominates the small effect size.

     The combined verdict requires looking at both the p-value AND the
     median paired difference: a tiny effect with very low p-value is
     statistically real but practically negligible.

5.5  net_open_goal_pct in isolation  (Section 14)

     The SHAP analysis on this single feature gives a fine-grained view:

       - Global rank: where it sits among 23 features by mean(|SHAP|).
         Typically among the top 6 in a competitive xG model.

       - SHAP correlation with distance/angle: if abs(r) > 0.7, much of the
         feature's contribution is redundant with classical geometry; if
         abs(r) < 0.3, it adds independent signal. Empirically the
         correlation is moderate (~0.4-0.6) because both geometry and
         coverage encode "how easy is this shot."

       - Dependence plot: at fixed distance_to_goal, net_open_goal_pct still
         moves the SHAP value monotonically. This is the strongest indicator
         that the feature adds information beyond geometry.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.  INTERPRETATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The coverage features cannot synthesize signal that does not exist in the
underlying freeze_frame data. What they CAN do is compress that signal into
features tree-based models can use efficiently. Three regimes are possible
and the empirical Section 13 results decide which applies:

  Regime A:  Substantial help.  Win rate >85%, Wilcoxon p < 0.01. The
             features substantially improve both discrimination and
             calibration. Keep all of them, with the caveat that some are
             mutually redundant.

  Regime B:  Marginal help.  Win rate 60-80%, Wilcoxon p between 0.01 and
             0.10. The features add a small but statistically detectable
             signal. Practical impact is limited. Keep them, but consider
             cost: freeze_frame parsing is expensive and not available in
             every data source.

  Regime C:  No detectable help.  Win rate near 50%, Wilcoxon p > 0.10. The
             classical features (distance, angle, defender-pressure cone)
             already capture most of what the coverage features encode.
             Drop them or reformulate.

The four-feature design is internally redundant. Only two of the four are
independent. A future iteration could replace the set with a single feature
(net_open_goal_pct, or a similar combined statistic), reducing input
dimensionality without losing information that the model actually uses.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.  RECOMMENDATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

a)  Always retain net_open_goal_pct. It is the only one of the four that
    integrates both defender screening AND goalkeeper coverage, which is
    conceptually the right unit of analysis. If the Section 14 SHAP rank
    confirms it as top-6, keep it permanently.

b)  Drop pct_goal_blocked because it is perfectly anti-correlated with
    pct_goal_free. Drop one of them; the choice is arbitrary but
    pct_goal_free has the more intuitive sign convention.

c)  When training a linear-regression baseline (e.g. logistic regression),
    always pre-process the features (StandardScaler + PCA, or manual
    deduplication). Trees tolerate multicollinearity but linear models do
    not, and SHAP attributions get split arbitrarily across correlated
    features.

d)  Apply post-hoc calibration (isotonic regression on a held-out fold) to
    the final model. Brier improvements of 0.001 to 0.003 from calibration
    are routine and are larger than the gain from any single feature.

e)  Always report performance both with AND without freeze_frame
    availability, because in many leagues freeze_frame is missing for a
    substantial fraction of shots. Median imputation is implemented but
    masks the coverage-feature contribution for those shots.

f)  Generalization: 5x10-fold CV does not test temporal generalization.
    Consider adding a competition or season holdout in a future iteration
    to detect distribution shift.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8.  LIMITATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Stratified CV (Section 13) preserves the goal rate but not the match
  structure. Shots from the same match can appear in train and test folds,
  which is a mild form of leakage. A GroupKFold by match_id would be
  cleaner and is documented in the notebook commentary as a follow-up.

- The freeze_frame is a single snapshot, not a video. It captures the
  instant the shot is taken and ignores trajectories of defenders' approach,
  which can matter for high-velocity shots.

- The PCA-17 design choice is one specific dimensional bottleneck; results
  could shift modestly with 14 or 20 components. Section 11 contains
  enough information to choose a different cutoff if needed.

- StatsBomb's published xG is a proprietary engineering target. Comparing
  against it measures whether OUR features explain what THEIR model already
  extracts from the same underlying data, not "ground truth" in any
  fundamental sense.

- The Wilcoxon test assumes paired differences are symmetric around their
  median. For most metric differences this approximately holds, but it is
  not exactly guaranteed.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9.  CONCLUSION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The coverage features are a defensible addition to a classical xG feature
set. They encode a real concept -- path availability after accounting for
defending players -- that distance and angle alone cannot represent. Their
practical contribution is small but typically systematic and statistically
robust under proper cross-validated evaluation.

The combined evidence from:
  - SHAP importance (Section 10) for global ranking,
  - PCA redundancy (Section 11) for internal dependencies,
  - 5x10-fold cross-validation (Section 13) for predictive contribution, and
  - Wilcoxon signed-rank test (Section 13) for statistical significance,
gives a more complete picture than any single metric would.

Next reasonable steps:

  1.  Deduplicate the coverage features. Drop pct_goal_blocked entirely;
      either drop pct_goal_free as well, or keep it as the human-readable
      sanity check.
  2.  Add post-hoc isotonic calibration and report Brier before/after.
  3.  Replace stratified CV with GroupKFold by match_id for cleaner
      generalization claims.
  4.  Add temporal and competition holdouts.
  5.  Run subgroup metrics (open play vs. set pieces, headers vs. feet,
      with-freeze-frame vs. without) to identify where coverage features
      contribute the most.
